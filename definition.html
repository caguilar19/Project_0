What is a Taco?

The answer isn't simple. One can define a taco by including three basic components:

  • A tortilla, used as the vessel for the rest of the components
  • A filling, usually some type of meat possibly accompanied by vegetables or herbs
  • A sauce
but this is oversimplifying the beautiful complexity of the dish. The truth is, we can't give an exact definition to a taco, and that's not a bad thing. The idea of using a tortilla as a vessel for food was around before the term existed and it's evolved since the term was first used. The food is dynamic beyond any definition. If we had stuck to a pure definition, 'taco al pastor' might never have been referred to as a taco, for it didn't line up with the miners' original dish.

The one thing that truly characterizes a taco is the simple, elegant, magical tortilla. The tortilla is the canvas for the taco, which is part of what makes a taco so special. A chef making a taco is an artist; they take a tortilla and apply their own style to creating their own taco—whether or not it would fall under the realm of 'traditional Mexican' has no effect on the status of the dish as a taco. By being such a simple dish, it opens up the doors to be truly creative, which has given rise to incredible gastronomical blending. From the Korean tacos at Kogi to the Lebanese influenced tacos al pastor in Mexico, tacos are truly a dish that transcends one culture. It's an expression, like chef Enrique Olvera said, whose versatility is what makes it so appealing. It's a work of art stemming from a simple base: a tortilla.

Claudio Aguilar
